Temptemus! (Let’s Try!)

Vergil: Aeneid, 2.162-170

Meter: dactylic hexameter

In book II of the Aeneid, Aeneas recounts the fate of Troy to Dido. In this passage, Aeneas tells of the speech of Sinon, who pretended to have abandoned the Greek cause and was left behind when the Greeks sailed away. In his deceitful speech explaining why he left the Greeks (whom he calls “Danaans”), he discusses the theft of the Palladium.

omnis spes Danaum et coepti fiducia belli
Palladis auxiliis semper stetit. Impius ex quo
Tydides sed enim scelerumque inventor Ulixes,
fatale adgressi sacrato avellere templo 165
Palladium caesis summae custodibus arcis,
corripuere sacram effigiem manibusque cruentis
virgineas ausi divae contingere vittas,
ex illo fluere ac retro sublapsa referri
spes Danaum, fractae vires; aversa deae mens 170

The entire hope of the Danaans and their faith in their undertaken war always stood firm with the help of Pallas. But indeed, from that time when wicked (Diomedes,) son of Tydeus, and the author of crimes, Odysseus, undertook to take away the fateful Palladium from the sacred temple with the guards of the highest citadel slaughtered, they dared to seize the sacred image and touch with bloody hands the pure garlands of the goddess. From that time on, the hopes of the Danaans fell, and having slipped back, withdrew, their strength shattered; the mind of the goddess is hostile.
